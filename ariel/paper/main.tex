\begin{abstract}
We present a model-based calibration of the Tully-Fisher relation (TFR) using
the full spiral-galaxy sample from the DESI Data Release 1 (DR1) Peculiar
Velocity survey.  The DR1 catalog contains 10,278 spiral galaxies drawn from
the Siena Galaxy Atlas.  We define the TFR phase-space variables
$x = \log_{10}(V_\text{rot}/100~\text{km\,s}^{-1})$ and $y = M_R$
(the $r$-band isophotal absolute magnitude at $SB=26$~mag\,arcsec$^{-2}$),
and fit a two-component Gaussian mixture model (GMM) to the observed
$(x,\,y)$ distribution to characterize the spiral population.
The dominant GMM component (weight $w=0.932$) defines the orientation of
oblique selection cuts in phase space; galaxies passing these cuts together
with a redshift window $0.03 < z < 0.1$ constitute the training sample of
5,928 galaxies.  We calibrate the TFR by fitting a Stan tophat model to
this sample via Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, obtaining slope
$a = -6.797 \pm 0.071$, intercept $b = -19.236 \pm 0.017$,
intrinsic scatter along the velocity axis $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x} = 0.026 \pm 0.003$,
and intrinsic scatter along the magnitude axis $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y} = 0.422 \pm 0.012$.
The calibrated model is applied to the full DR1 spiral sample to produce a
peculiar velocity catalog (\texttt{tophat\_catalog.fits}) and a full posterior
predictive covariance matrix (\texttt{tophat\_cov.fits}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Peculiar velocities --- the deviations of galaxy redshifts from pure Hubble flow ---
encode the gravitational influence of large-scale structure and provide an independent
probe of the growth rate of density perturbations, bulk flows, and the Hubble constant
\citep[e.g.][]{1997AJ....113...53G, 2008ApJ...676..184T, 2019MNRAS.487.2061H}.
The Tully-Fisher relation \citep[TFR;][]{Tully1977} links the intrinsic luminosity of
a spiral galaxy to its rotation velocity.
Because more luminous spirals rotate faster, a consequence of the dark-matter halos
that govern galaxy dynamics \citep{1998MNRAS.295..319M},
the TFR provides a distance estimate independent of redshift: the luminosity is inferred
from the rotation velocity, and the distance from the luminosity--flux ratio.
Peculiar velocity surveys based on the TFR have been carried out by a number of groups
\citep[e.g.][]{2000ApJ...529..698S, 2008ApJ...676..184T, 2019MNRAS.487.2061H,
2020MNRAS.497.1275S, 2020ApJ...902..145K}.

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument \citep[DESI;][]{cite_desi} is now delivering
the largest spectroscopic peculiar-velocity dataset to date.
The DESI Peculiar Velocity survey \citep{Saulder2023} targets spiral galaxies,
early-type galaxies (Fundamental Plane), and supernova hosts.
A first TFR catalog of 1,106 spirals from the DESI Early Data Release
\citep[EDR;][]{DESI_EDR} was presented in \citet{2024Douglass}, with particular depth
in the Coma cluster region.
Data Release 1 (DR1) extends the spiral sample to 10,278 galaxies spanning the full DESI
footprint, forming the dataset analyzed here.

Traditional TFR calibrations fit a line to the data after applying iterative
bias corrections for selection effects and measurement errors
\citep{2006ApJ...653..861M, 2012ApJ...749...78T, 1997AJ....113...53G}.
We adopt instead a forward-modeling approach: we write down a probabilistic galaxy
model that contains the TFR as its core relationship, and calibrate it by maximizing
the likelihood of the observations.
This has several advantages over the traditional approach.
Measurement uncertainties in both the rotation velocity and magnitude, including the
intrinsic scatter of the TFR, enter the likelihood directly without the need for
separate bias corrections.
The selection function is folded into the likelihood, so no iterative reweighting of
the sample is required.
Both the TFR slope and intercept and the intrinsic scatter parameters are determined
in a single joint fit.
This modeling approach does require specifying the intrinsic distribution of galaxy
rotation velocities; we address this by fitting a Gaussian mixture model to the
observed phase space and adopting a uniform (tophat) prior within the selected region
(\S\ref{sec:model}--\ref{sec:selection}).

In this paper we present a model-based calibration of the TFR using the DESI DR1
spiral sample.
We describe the sample selection criteria (\S\ref{sec:sampleselection}),
the Stan tophat model and its likelihood (\S\ref{sec:model}),
the GMM-based phase-space selection function (\S\ref{sec:selection}),
the MCMC fit results and diagnostics (\S\ref{sec:results}),
and the output peculiar velocity catalog (\S\ref{sec:catalog}).


\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
The DESI DR1 Peculiar Velocity spiral sample contains 10,278 galaxies drawn from the Siena Galaxy Atlas \citep[SGA;][]{SGA}, which is based on the DESI Legacy Imaging Surveys DR9 data \citep{DESI_Imaging}, covering areas north of $-30^\circ$ declination. The SGA includes galaxies with diameters larger than 20'' at the 25th
magnitude isophote, denoted as $D(25)$. To be part of the TFR sample, a galaxy must have a Sersic index less than 2, indicating it is likely a spiral galaxy, and an inclination angle greater than $25^\circ$, ensuring a significant portion of the galaxy's rotational velocity is visible and the galaxy's major axis is properly aligned.

SGA provides galaxy shape and magnitude information
at the $r$-band  $SB=26$~$\text{mag}\,\text{arcsec}^{-2}$ isophote, in the form of the photometric axis ratio $b/a$
(not to be confused with the $a$ and $b$ of the TFR),
the position angle PA of the semi-major axis,
and the radius $R_{26}$.  The SGA
isophotal magnitude is denoted $R$.

The DESI measurements are taken at
the galactic center and on both sides along the major axis oriented according to the SGA PA.
Rotational velocities were measured at $0.33R_{26}$,
the distance at which reliable redshifts could consistently be measured.  The redshifts measured at different positions
are combined to obtain a redshift $z$ and measured
rotational velocity $V_\text{obs}$ for each galaxy
\citep{2024Douglass}.

SGA and DESI data are combined to infer the rotation
velocity $V_\text{rot}$.
The galaxy inclination angle $i$ is derived from
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:BAtoi}
    \cos^2 i = \frac{(b/a)^2 - q_0^2}{1 - q_0^2},
\end{equation}
where $q_0 = 0.2$ \citep{Tully2000}.
This in
turn is used to convert the observed line-of-sight velocity to the
the rotation velocity as
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:Vcorrected}
    V_{\text{rot}} = \frac{V_{\text{obs}}}{\sin i}.
\end{equation}

The analysis variables used throughout this paper are
\begin{align}
    x &= \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{V_\text{rot}}{100~\text{km\,s}^{-1}}\right), \\
    y &= M_R,
\end{align}
where $M_R$ is the $r$-band SB26 absolute magnitude computed from
the apparent magnitude $R$ and the luminosity distance at the observed redshift.

The above equation for $V_\text{rot}$ applies when the fiber is
positioned on the
semi-major axis, which
is perpendicular to the rotation velocity.
DESI fibers are placed according to the PA's from SGA,
which are based
on imaging.  Galaxy velocity maps, however,
better determine
the axis where line-of-sight rotation
velocities are maximized.
We use the subset of our galaxies also observed by
SDSS Mapping Nearby Galaxies at APO Survey \citep[MaNGA][]{MaNGA} in SDSS MaNGA DR17 \citep{SDSS_DR17}
and modeled by
\cite{Ravi2023} to estimate
the SGA PA uncertainties.

\section{Sample Selection}
\label{sec:sampleselection}
Not all galaxies in the DR1 spiral catalog are suitable for TFR calibration.
Dwarf and irregular galaxies at the faint end of the luminosity function do
not follow the same TFR as normal spirals, while extreme outliers at either
end of the phase-space distribution can disproportionately influence the fit.
We therefore restrict the analysis to a region $\mathcal{S}$ in the observed
$(\hat{x},\hat{y})$ plane that selects the well-behaved spiral population.

The region $\mathcal{S}$ is defined by two types of cut.
First, upper and lower limits on the observed absolute magnitude $\hat{y}$
exclude the faintest galaxies, which depart from the TFR, and the
brightest galaxies, which are rare and susceptible to outlier contamination.
Second, two diagonal bands oriented approximately parallel to the TFR --- and therefore
perpendicular to the direction of TFR scatter --- bound the sample on either
side of the main locus.
Cutting parallel to the relation rather than in $\hat{y}$ or $\hat{x}$ alone
efficiently encloses the TFR population across the full range of rotation
velocities and avoids sculpting the scatter distribution asymmetrically.
Together the four boundaries define a parallelogram-shaped region,
\begin{equation}
(\hat{x},\hat{y})\in\mathcal{S}
\;\Longleftrightarrow\;
\hat{y}_{\min} \le \hat{y} \le \hat{y}_{\max}
\;\text{ and }\;
s_{\text{plane}}\,\hat{x}+c_1 \le \hat{y} \le s_{\text{plane}}\,\hat{x}+c_2,
\label{eqn:selection_region}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{y}_{\min}$ and $\hat{y}_{\max}$ are the magnitude limits,
$s_{\text{plane}}$ is the slope of the oblique boundaries,
$c_1 < c_2$ are their intercepts, and $d\equiv c_2-c_1$ is the
perpendicular band width.


The TFR is unknown a priori, so the diagonal band is based on an initial estimate
of the slope of the distribution.  Variations in the selected slope perturb
the galaxies that enter the analysis, but otherwise do not bias the fits.
The procedure for selecting the limits is dependent
on the model and is presented in \S\ref{sec:selection}.

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}
We construct a probabilistic forward model for the TFR that accounts for
measurement uncertainties in both observables and for sample selection within
a single likelihood.
The full derivation is given in Appendix~\ref{sec:likelihood};
here we state the key elements and working likelihood expressions.

The sample satisfies the selection criteria
$(\hat{x}_i,\hat{y}_i) \in \mathcal{S}$.
The training sample contains $N$ galaxies.
For each galaxy $i$, the observables are the measured log-velocity
$\hat{x}_i$ with uncertainty $\sigma_{x,i}$ and the observed absolute
magnitude $\hat{y}_i$ with uncertainty $\sigma_{y,i}$.
Velocity uncertainties are treated as Gaussian in log-space.

The global TFR parameters are the slope $a$, intercept $b$, and intrinsic
scatter amplitudes $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x}$ and $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y}$
along the $x$- and $y$-axes respectively.
Each galaxy $i$ has a latent on-TFR magnitude $y_{\mathrm{TF},i}$ that
places it on the relation.   The true absolute magnitude and
velocities $x_i$ and $y_i$
have scatter around the TFR,
\begin{align}
y_i \mid y_{\mathrm{TF},i}
    &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(y_{\mathrm{TF},i},\,\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y}^2\right), \\
x_i \mid y_{\mathrm{TF},i}
    &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\frac{y_{\mathrm{TF},i}-b}{a},\,\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x}^2\right).
\end{align}
Measurements have Gaussian scatter around the true values:
\begin{align}
\hat{x}_i \mid x_i &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_i,\,\sigma_{x,i}^2\right), \\
\hat{y}_i \mid y_i &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(y_i,\,\sigma_{y,i}^2\right).
\end{align}

For a hierarchical model, the underlying distribution of the latent
parameter must be specified.  Here we adopt 
a top-hat distribution of $y_{\mathrm{TF}}$ characterised by
bounds $y_{\min}$ and $y_{\max}$.
\begin{align}
y_{\mathrm{TF},i} &\sim \mathrm{Uniform}(y_{\min},\,y_{\max}).
\end{align}
Normal distributions were also considered,
but studies using mock catalogs showed that the top-hat better described
the flat-cuspy distribution of galaxy absolute magnitudes.

The likelihood for this model is as follows.
Given the independence of that galaxy measurements, the total likelihood can be decomposed
into the product of per-galaxy terms. 
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}(\theta;\{\hat{x}_i\},\{\hat{y}_i\})
&= \prod_{i=1}^{N} p(\hat{x}_i,\hat{y}_i \mid \theta, S_i=1).
\label{eqn:total_likelihood}
\end{align}
Convolving intrinsic and measurement scatter defines the combined uncertainties
\begin{align}
\sigma_{1,i}^2 &= \sigma_{x,i}^2+\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x}^2, &
\sigma_{2,i}^2 &= \sigma_{y,i}^2+\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y}^2.
\label{eqn:sigma12}
\end{align}

Because the sample is restricted to $(\hat{x}_i,\hat{y}_i)\in\mathcal{S}$,
the per-galaxy density is truncated and renormalised,
\begin{align}
p(\hat{x}_i,\hat{y}_i \mid \theta, S_i=1)
&= \frac{\mathcal{L}_i\;
         \mathbf{1}\!\left\{(\hat{x}_i,\hat{y}_i)\in\mathcal{S}\right\}}
        {P(S_i=1\mid\theta)},
\label{eqn:selected_density}
\end{align}
where
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_i
&= \frac{|a|}{y_{\max}-y_{\min}}\;
   \mathcal{N}\!\left(\hat{y}_i;\,b+a\hat{x}_i,\,\sigma_{\mathrm{tot},i}^2\right)
   \left[
     \Phi\!\left(\frac{y_{\max}-\mu_{*,i}}{\sigma_{*,i}}\right)
     -\Phi\!\left(\frac{y_{\min}-\mu_{*,i}}{\sigma_{*,i}}\right)
   \right],
\label{eqn:Li_tophat}
\end{align}
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF and
\begin{align}
\sigma_{\mathrm{tot},i}^2
    &= a^2\sigma_{1,i}^2+\sigma_{2,i}^2, \\
\mu_{*,i}
    &= \frac{(a\hat{x}_i+b)\,\sigma_{2,i}^2
             +\hat{y}_i\,a^2\sigma_{1,i}^2}{\sigma_{\mathrm{tot},i}^2}, \\
\sigma_{*,i}^2
    &= \frac{a^2\sigma_{1,i}^2\,\sigma_{2,i}^2}{\sigma_{\mathrm{tot},i}^2}.
\label{eqn:posterior_params}
\end{align}
is the per-galaxy likelihood if there were no  sample selection.

The selection probability is
\begin{align}
P(S_i=1\mid\theta)
&= \frac{1}{y_{\max}-y_{\min}}
   \int_{y_{\min}}^{y_{\max}} I_i(y_{\mathrm{TF}})\,dy_{\mathrm{TF}},
\label{eqn:Psel_tophat}
\end{align}
where $I_i$ is a combination of four bivariate-normal CDF evaluations,
\begin{align}
I_i(y_{\mathrm{TF}})
&\equiv
  \Phi_2\!\Bigl(h_i(0;y_{\mathrm{TF}}),\;k_i(\hat{y}_{\max};y_{\mathrm{TF}});\;{-\rho_i}\Bigr)
 -\Phi_2\!\Bigl(h_i(d;y_{\mathrm{TF}}),\;k_i(\hat{y}_{\max};y_{\mathrm{TF}});\;{-\rho_i}\Bigr)
\nonumber\\
&\quad
 -\Phi_2\!\Bigl(h_i(0;y_{\mathrm{TF}}),\;k_i(\hat{y}_{\min};y_{\mathrm{TF}});\;{-\rho_i}\Bigr)
 +\Phi_2\!\Bigl(h_i(d;y_{\mathrm{TF}}),\;k_i(\hat{y}_{\min};y_{\mathrm{TF}});\;{-\rho_i}\Bigr),
\label{eqn:Ii_def}
\end{align}
with $\Phi_2(h,k;\varrho)$ the standard bivariate-normal CDF,
$d\equiv c_2-c_1$, and the standardised bounds
\begin{align}
h_i(t;y_{\mathrm{TF}}) &\equiv \frac{\mu_{u,i}(y_{\mathrm{TF}})-t}{\sigma_{u,i}}, &
k_i(y_\star;y_{\mathrm{TF}}) &\equiv \frac{y_\star - y_{\mathrm{TF}}}{\sigma_{2,i}}.
\label{eqn:ab_bounds}
\end{align}
Here $\mu_{u,i}$, $\sigma_{u,i}$, and $\rho_i$ are the mean, standard deviation,
and correlation of the sheared observable $\hat{u}_i\equiv\hat{y}_i - s_\text{plane}\hat{x}_i - c_1$
conditional on $y_{\mathrm{TF}}$:
\begin{align}
\mu_{u,i}(y_{\mathrm{TF}})
    &= y_{\mathrm{TF}} - s_\text{plane}\,\frac{y_{\mathrm{TF}}-b}{a} - c_1, \\
\sigma_{u,i}^2
    &= \sigma_{2,i}^2 + s_\text{plane}^2\,\sigma_{1,i}^2, \\
\rho_i
    &= \frac{\sigma_{2,i}}{\sigma_{u,i}}.
\label{eqn:shear_params}
\end{align}
The above is derived by transforming the $\hat{x}$--$\hat{y}$ to a new coordinate system where
the parallelogram transforms into an axis-aligned rectangle.  The rectangular selection region
is described by the sums and differences of four half planes.  The selection probability of
a half plane is the standard bivariate-normal CDF.

\section{Selection Function}
\label{sec:selection}
The DR1 spiral catalog spans a broad range of galaxy properties, including a
dwarf-galaxy population that does not conform to the standard spiral TFR.
Rather than applying simple velocity and magnitude cuts, we define the
training sample using an oblique selection boundary in $(x,\,y)$ phase space
whose orientation is informed by the data themselves via a Gaussian mixture
model (GMM) fit.

\subsection{Phase-Space Distribution}
\label{sec:gmm}
We fit a two-component GMM to the $(x,\,y)$ distribution of the full
10,278-galaxy DR1 catalog.  The dominant component represents the TFR
spiral population; the secondary component captures outliers and dwarf galaxies.

The best-fit GMM yields a dominant-component weight of $w = 0.932$.
The mean of the dominant component is
$(\bar{x},\,\bar{y}) = (0.185,\,-20.530)$,
with semi-axes $[0.064,\, 0.930]$ and orientation angle $98.4^\circ$
measured from the $x$-axis.
The elongated ellipse with a nearly vertical orientation reflects the
steep slope of the TFR: the long axis of the component is closely aligned
with the TFR direction in $(x,\,y)$ space.

The GMM ellipses overlaid on the phase-space scatter are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:selection_ellipse}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/selection_ellipse.png}
    \caption{Distribution of DR1 spiral galaxies in $(x,\,y)$ phase space,
    where $x = \log_{10}(V_\text{rot}/100~\text{km\,s}^{-1})$ and $y = M_R$.
    The two GMM ellipses (at $1\sigma$) are overplotted; the dominant component

\subsection{Oblique Selection Cuts}
\label{sec:oblique}
The dominant GMM ellipse defines a preferred direction in $(x,\,y)$ space.
We use this direction to construct oblique (tilted) selection boundaries that
are parallel to the TFR and perpendicular to the scatter axis.

The two boundaries are:
\begin{align}
    y &\geq s_\text{plane}\, x + c_1 \quad \text{(lower boundary)}, \\
    y &\leq s_\text{plane}\, x + c_2 \quad \text{(upper boundary)},
\end{align}
where $s_\text{plane}$ is fixed by the GMM ellipse orientation, and
$c_1$, $c_2$ are determined by the parameter $n_{\sigma,\perp}$,
the number of perpendicular $\sigma$ from the ellipse center to each boundary.

In addition to the oblique cuts, a magnitude window
$\hat{y}_\text{min} \leq y \leq \hat{y}_\text{max}$ is imposed to remove
the brightest and faintest extremes of the distribution, and a redshift window
$z_\text{min} < z < z_\text{max}$ is applied to the training sample only
(the full sample without $z$ cuts is used for the peculiar velocity catalog).

The full sample and the selected training region are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:data}.

\subsection{Fiducial Selection Parameters}
\label{sec:fiducial}

calibration, we run Stan MLE on the $n_{\sigma,\perp} = 3$ ellipse and
examine the pull profile (normalized residual as a function of position in
phase space).  A flat pull profile confirms that the selection does not
preferentially bias the sample in any region of phase space.
The pull profile for the fiducial selection is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:pull}, and is consistent with zero across the full range.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{MCMC Fit}
\label{sec:mcmc}
The model is fit to the 5,928-galaxy training sample using Hamiltonian Monte
Carlo as implemented in Stan \citep{STAN_html} via the CmdStan interface.
We run 4 chains, each with 1000 warm-up and 1000 sampling iterations.
Two chains converge for the DR1 dataset; the combined posterior uses
2,000 samples.  The mean acceptance rate is $0.938$ and there are no
divergences.

The posterior 68\% confidence intervals for the global TFR parameters are
given in Table~\ref{tab:results}.  The full posterior corner plot is shown
in Fig.~\ref{fig:tophat}.

\section{Peculiar Velocity Catalog}
\label{sec:catalog}
The calibrated tophat model is applied to all galaxies in the MAIN sample
(those satisfying the magnitude and oblique cuts without the redshift
restriction) to produce a peculiar velocity catalog.

\subsection{Output Files}
The catalog is provided as \texttt{tophat\_catalog.fits}, which augments the
input FITS table with five new columns:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{MU\_TF}: the TFR distance modulus residual,
    $\hat{m}_\text{obs} - \bar{m}_\text{pred}$.
    \item \texttt{MU\_ERR}: the combined uncertainty on \texttt{MU\_TF},
    propagated from measurement and intrinsic scatter.
    \item \texttt{LOGDIST}: the log-distance estimate,
    $0.2 \times (\hat{m}_\text{obs} - M_\text{TF})$.
    \item \texttt{LOGDIST\_ERR}: the uncertainty on \texttt{LOGDIST}.
    \item \texttt{MAIN}: a boolean flag indicating whether the galaxy
    satisfies the magnitude and oblique cuts (without redshift restriction).
\end{itemize}

The full posterior predictive covariance matrix among all MAIN-sample
galaxies is provided as \texttt{tophat\_cov.fits}.  This $N\times N$
matrix encodes the correlated uncertainties arising from the shared
TFR calibration parameters and is required for cosmological analyses
that use the full peculiar velocity field.  The covariance matrix
structure is visualized in Fig.~\ref{fig:tophat_cov}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/tophat_cov.png}
    \caption{Visualization of the posterior predictive covariance matrix

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
We have presented a model-based calibration of the Tully-Fisher relation
using the full spiral-galaxy sample from the DESI DR1 Peculiar Velocity survey.
The key results are as follows.
\begin{itemize}
    \item A two-component GMM fit to the DR1 phase space identifies the
    dominant spiral TFR population (weight $w = 0.932$) and defines the
    orientation of oblique selection cuts.  The fiducial selection yields
    a training sample of 5,928 galaxies with a flat pull profile confirming
    no selection-induced bias.
    \item The Stan tophat model, which places a uniform prior on the latent
    log-velocity within the observed selection region, correctly accounts for
    the complex oblique selection boundary without the need for explicit
    bias corrections.
    \item MCMC posterior parameters are slope $a = -6.797 \pm 0.071$,
    intercept $b = -19.236 \pm 0.017$, and intrinsic scatter
    $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x} = 0.026 \pm 0.003$,
    $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y} = 0.422 \pm 0.012$.
    Residual diagnostics in both phase space and redshift show no evidence
    for systematic trends.
    \item The calibrated model is applied to produce a peculiar velocity
    catalog (\texttt{tophat\_catalog.fits}) and a posterior predictive
    covariance matrix (\texttt{tophat\_cov.fits}) for the full MAIN sample.
\end{itemize}

This work is distinguished from other approaches in that it fits a TFR model
rather than fitting a TFR to data.  The difference is highlighted in that
here parameter estimation is based on the model likelihood whereas in other
work a reasonable yet arbitrary merit function is optimized.  A feature of
the TFR model is that it must specify the latent parameter distribution, upon
which we find that the results are sensitive.  An advantage of our approach
is that the model can be used to analyze independent datasets in a
self-consistent manner.

Peculiar velocity measurements from the MAIN catalog, including the study
of large-scale flows and comparison with existing peculiar velocity compilations,
will be presented in a follow-up paper.
