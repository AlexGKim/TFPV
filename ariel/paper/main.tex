\begin{abstract}
We present a model-based calibration of the Tully-Fisher relation (TFR) using
the full spiral-galaxy sample from the DESI Data Release 1 (DR1) Peculiar
Velocity survey.  The DR1 catalog contains 10,278 spiral galaxies drawn from
the Siena Galaxy Atlas.  We define the TFR phase-space variables
$x = \log_{10}(V_\text{rot}/100~\text{km\,s}^{-1})$ and $y = M_R$
(the $r$-band isophotal absolute magnitude at $SB=26$~mag\,arcsec$^{-2}$),
and fit a two-component Gaussian mixture model (GMM) to the observed
$(x,\,y)$ distribution to characterize the spiral population.
The dominant GMM component (weight $w=0.932$) defines the orientation of
oblique selection cuts in phase space; galaxies passing these cuts together
with a redshift window $0.03 < z < 0.1$ constitute the training sample of
5,928 galaxies.  We calibrate the TFR by fitting a Stan tophat model to
this sample via Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, obtaining slope
$a = -6.797 \pm 0.071$, intercept $b = -19.236 \pm 0.017$,
intrinsic scatter along the velocity axis $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x} = 0.026 \pm 0.003$,
and intrinsic scatter along the magnitude axis $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y} = 0.422 \pm 0.012$.
The calibrated model is applied to the full DR1 spiral sample to produce a
peculiar velocity catalog (\texttt{tophat\_catalog.fits}) and a full posterior
predictive covariance matrix (\texttt{tophat\_cov.fits}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
The Tully-Fisher relation
\citep[TFR;][]{Tully1977} describes an empirical correlation that links
the intrinsic brightness of spiral galaxies to their rotational velocities.
Higher luminosity spiral galaxies exhibit discs with higher rotational speeds,
a correlation that
can be explained by the underlying dark matter halos that surround galaxies
\citep{1998MNRAS.295..319M}.
The distance to a spiral can then be inferred given measurements
of rotation velocity and flux: the galaxy luminosity is inferred
from the velocity and TFR, and the distance can be inferred from the luminosity--flux ratio.
Spiral galaxies, through the TFR, have served as popular
distance indicators actively collected and analyzed by a number of surveys \citep[e.g.][]{1997AJ....113...53G, 2000ApJ...529..698S, 2008ApJ...676..184T,1998MNRAS.295..319M, 2019MNRAS.487.2061H,
2020MNRAS.497.1275S,2020ApJ...902..145K}.

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) collaboration
is executing such a survey.
DESI is using the Kitt Peak Mayall 4-m Telescope
instrumented with a multi-object spectrograph to observe galaxies,
quasars, and other sources using a survey strategy that is now
delivering precision measurements of the growth of structure [cite].
A Peculiar Velocity survey, described in  \cite{Saulder2023},  targets
supernova hosts, Fundamental plane galaxies, and spirals
and is a component of the larger survey.

The first suite of DESI spiral-galaxy and TFR measurements using the Early Data Release
\citep{DESI_EDR} are reported in \citet{2024Douglass}.
There, the first catalog of 1106 TFR-based peculiar velocity measurements from DESI
was presented, with deep measurements obtained in and around the Coma cluster.
The Data Release 1 (DR1) expands the spiral sample to 10,278 galaxies covering the
full DESI footprint, and is the focus of the present analysis.

The TFR relation takes the form
\begin{align}
    y = b + a\, x,
\end{align}
where $y = M_R$ is the absolute magnitude, $x = \log_{10}(V_\text{rot}/100~\text{km\,s}^{-1})$
is the log rotation velocity relative to $100~\text{km\,s}^{-1}$, and $a$ and $b$ are the
TFR slope and zero point, respectively.
In previous work, data were bias-corrected to account for non-trivial observational
features and sample selection \citep{2006ApJ...653..861M}, and
$a$ and $b$ are fit by minimizing a least-squares
merit function \citep{2012ApJ...749...78T, 1997AJ....113...53G}. Some analyses ran multiple iterations in order to refine their sample selection.

In this work we present a different approach toward calibrating the TFR. Rather than fitting a line (or curve) to the data, we adopt a galaxy model that contains an intrinsic TFR and
fit for the TFR parameters. The advantages of this model-fitting approach include
%
\begin{itemize}
    \item Ability to simultaneously consider measurement uncertainty that is Gaussian in velocity with intrinsic dispersion Gaussian in log-velocity.
    \item Incorporation of velocity measurement error due to imperfect observations (in our case the fiber not correctly placed along the galaxy disc).
    \item Sample selection incorporated into the likelihood without
    the need for bias corrections.
    \item Convergence to a solution with a single fit.
    \item Fitting of nuisance parameters. The TFR exhibits scatter larger than that expected from measurement uncertainty, which is attributed to intrinsic dispersion.  The intrinsic dispersion and its axis can be modeled and fitted in the analysis.
    \item The model and the fit results from one dataset can be applied
    to a different dataset.
\end{itemize}
%
A requirement of the modeling approach is that the intrinsic velocity distribution must be specified.

In this article we present a model-based calibration of the
TFR using the full spiral-galaxy sample from the DESI DR1 Peculiar Velocity survey.
We describe the GMM-based selection function used to define the training sample
(\S\ref{sec:selection}), the Stan tophat model and its likelihood
(\S\ref{sec:model}), the MCMC fit results and diagnostics (\S\ref{sec:results}),
and the output peculiar velocity catalog (\S\ref{sec:catalog}).


\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
The DESI DR1 Peculiar Velocity spiral sample contains 10,278 galaxies drawn from the Siena Galaxy Atlas \citep[SGA;][]{SGA}, which is based on the DESI Legacy Imaging Surveys DR9 data \citep{DESI_Imaging}, covering areas north of $-30^\circ$ declination. The SGA includes galaxies with diameters larger than 20'' at the 25th
magnitude isophote, denoted as $D(25)$. To be part of the TFR sample, a galaxy must have a Sersic index less than 2, indicating it is likely a spiral galaxy, and an inclination angle greater than $25^\circ$, ensuring a significant portion of the galaxy's rotational velocity is visible and the galaxy's major axis is properly aligned.

SGA provides galaxy shape and magnitude information
at the $r$-band  $SB=26$~$\text{mag}\,\text{arcsec}^{-2}$ isophote, in the form of the photometric axis ratio $b/a$
(not to be confused with the $a$ and $b$ of the TFR),
the position angle PA of the semi-major axis,
and the radius $R_{26}$.  The SGA
isophotal magnitude is denoted $R$.

The redshift and $R$ isophotal magnitude of the DR1 sample galaxies are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:zR}.  The SGA has a $D(25)>0.333'$
sample-selection criterion, which translates to a fuzzy edge
in the $R$ magnitude.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    %\includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/zR.png}
    \fbox{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\centering\vspace{3cm}[zR.png --- redshift vs.\ apparent magnitude scatter plot]\vspace{3cm}}}
    \caption{Redshifts and $R$ isophotal magnitudes of the full DESI DR1 TFR sample.}
    \label{fig:zR}
\end{figure}

The DESI measurements are taken at
the galactic center and on both sides along the major axis oriented according to the SGA PA.
Rotational velocities were measured at $0.33R_{26}$,
the distance at which reliable redshifts could consistently be measured.  The redshifts measured at different positions
are combined to obtain a redshift $z$ and measured
rotational velocity $V_\text{obs}$ for each galaxy
\citep{2024Douglass}.

SGA and DESI data are combined to infer the rotation
velocity $V_\text{rot}$.
The galaxy inclination angle $i$ is derived from
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:BAtoi}
    \cos^2 i = \frac{(b/a)^2 - q_0^2}{1 - q_0^2},
\end{equation}
where $q_0 = 0.2$ \citep{Tully2000}.
This in
turn is used to convert the observed line-of-sight velocity to the
the rotation velocity as
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:Vcorrected}
    V_{\text{rot}} = \frac{V_{\text{obs}}}{\sin i}.
\end{equation}

The analysis variables used throughout this paper are
\begin{align}
    x &= \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{V_\text{rot}}{100~\text{km\,s}^{-1}}\right), \\
    y &= M_R,
\end{align}
where $M_R$ is the $r$-band SB26 absolute magnitude computed from
the apparent magnitude $R$ and the luminosity distance at the observed redshift.

The above equation for $V_\text{rot}$ applies when the fiber is
positioned on the
semi-major axis, which
is perpendicular to the rotation velocity.
DESI fibers are placed according to the PA's from SGA,
which are based
on imaging.  Galaxy velocity maps, however,
better determine
the axis where line-of-sight rotation
velocities are maximized.
We use the subset of our galaxies also observed by
SDSS Mapping Nearby Galaxies at APO Survey \citep[MaNGA][]{MaNGA} in SDSS MaNGA DR17 \citep{SDSS_DR17}
and modeled by
\cite{Ravi2023} to estimate
the SGA PA uncertainties.

The distribution of the differences between MaNGA and SGA PA's is shown
in Fig.~\ref{fig:Dpa}.
The distribution is modeled
as a Cauchy distribution with
a best-fit $\gamma=4.217^\circ$,
which we adopt as the uncertainty in the SGA PA measurement.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    %\includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/DPA.png}
    \fbox{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\centering\vspace{3cm}[DPA.png --- PA difference histogram]\vspace{3cm}}}
    \caption{Distribution
    of the differences between the PA determined from MaNGA and
    SGA for galaxies in both
    samples.  Fitting to a Cauchy distribution gives a best-fit $\gamma=4.217^\circ$, overplotted with the solid orange curve.}
    \label{fig:Dpa}
\end{figure}


\section{Selection Function}
\label{sec:selection}
The DR1 spiral catalog spans a broad range of galaxy properties, including a
dwarf-galaxy population that does not conform to the standard spiral TFR.
Rather than applying simple velocity and magnitude cuts, we define the
training sample using an oblique selection boundary in $(x,\,y)$ phase space
whose orientation is informed by the data themselves via a Gaussian mixture
model (GMM) fit.

\subsection{Phase-Space Distribution}
\label{sec:gmm}
We fit a two-component GMM to the $(x,\,y)$ distribution of the full
10,278-galaxy DR1 catalog.  The dominant component represents the TFR
spiral population; the secondary component captures outliers and dwarf galaxies.

The best-fit GMM yields a dominant-component weight of $w = 0.932$.
The mean of the dominant component is
$(\bar{x},\,\bar{y}) = (0.185,\,-20.530)$,
with semi-axes $[0.064,\, 0.930]$ and orientation angle $98.4^\circ$
measured from the $x$-axis.
The elongated ellipse with a nearly vertical orientation reflects the
steep slope of the TFR: the long axis of the component is closely aligned
with the TFR direction in $(x,\,y)$ space.

The GMM ellipses overlaid on the phase-space scatter are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:selection_ellipse}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/selection_ellipse.png}
    \caption{Distribution of DR1 spiral galaxies in $(x,\,y)$ phase space,
    where $x = \log_{10}(V_\text{rot}/100~\text{km\,s}^{-1})$ and $y = M_R$.
    The two GMM ellipses (at $1\sigma$) are overplotted; the dominant component
    (weight $w = 0.932$) is shown in orange.  The orientation of the dominant
    ellipse defines the slope of the oblique selection cuts described in
    \S\ref{sec:oblique}.}
    \label{fig:selection_ellipse}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Oblique Selection Cuts}
\label{sec:oblique}
The dominant GMM ellipse defines a preferred direction in $(x,\,y)$ space.
We use this direction to construct oblique (tilted) selection boundaries that
are parallel to the TFR and perpendicular to the scatter axis.

The two boundaries are:
\begin{align}
    y &\geq s_\text{plane}\, x + c_1 \quad \text{(lower boundary)}, \\
    y &\leq s_\text{plane}\, x + c_2 \quad \text{(upper boundary)},
\end{align}
where $s_\text{plane}$ is fixed by the GMM ellipse orientation, and
$c_1$, $c_2$ are determined by the parameter $n_{\sigma,\perp}$,
the number of perpendicular $\sigma$ from the ellipse center to each boundary.

In addition to the oblique cuts, a magnitude window
$\hat{y}_\text{min} \leq y \leq \hat{y}_\text{max}$ is imposed to remove
the brightest and faintest extremes of the distribution, and a redshift window
$z_\text{min} < z < z_\text{max}$ is applied to the training sample only
(the full sample without $z$ cuts is used for the peculiar velocity catalog).

The full sample and the selected training region are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:data}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/data.png}
    \caption{Full DR1 spiral sample (grey) and training sample (colored points)
    in $(x,\,y)$ phase space.  The oblique selection boundaries and magnitude
    limits are indicated.  The training sample contains 5,928 galaxies.}
    \label{fig:data}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Fiducial Selection Parameters}
\label{sec:fiducial}
To verify that the fiducial selection does not introduce bias in the TFR
calibration, we run Stan MLE on the $n_{\sigma,\perp} = 3$ ellipse and
examine the pull profile (normalized residual as a function of position in
phase space).  A flat pull profile confirms that the selection does not
preferentially bias the sample in any region of phase space.
The pull profile for the fiducial selection is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:pull}, and is consistent with zero across the full range.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/select_v2_pull.png}
    \caption{Pull profile (normalized mean residual) as a function of $x$
    for the fiducial selection.  The profile is consistent with zero,
    confirming that the oblique selection does not bias the TFR calibration.}
    \label{fig:pull}
\end{figure}

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}
We model the TFR using a Stan implementation of a tophat model.
The model is described in detail in \S\ref{sec:likelihood};
here we summarize its key elements.

The analysis variables for galaxy $i$ are the observed log-velocity
$\hat{x}_i$ with measurement uncertainty $\sigma_{x,i}$
and the observed absolute magnitude $\hat{y}_i$ with measurement uncertainty
$\sigma_{y,i}$.
The TFR is
\begin{equation}
    y = a\, x + b + \varepsilon_y,
\end{equation}
where $a$ is the slope, $b$ the intercept, and
$\varepsilon_y \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\mathrm{int},y})$ is intrinsic
scatter along the $y$ axis.  The true log-velocity $x$ has additional
intrinsic scatter $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x}$ about the TFR locus.

The latent true log-velocity $\hat{x}_\text{true}$ is drawn from a uniform
(tophat) prior over the observed $x$ range that satisfies the oblique
selection boundaries.  This choice correctly accounts for the truncation
imposed by the selection function without introducing a parametric assumption
about the intrinsic galaxy velocity function.

The velocity measurement uncertainty includes a contribution from imperfect
fiber placement along the galaxy major axis.  The offset $\delta\phi$ between
the DESI fiber position angle and the true galaxy major axis is modeled as
\begin{equation}
    \delta\phi \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,\, \gamma=4.217^\circ),
\end{equation}
which propagates to an additional multiplicative factor $\sec(\delta\phi)$ in
the observed rotation velocity.

The MAIN sample used for the peculiar velocity catalog is defined by the
same magnitude and oblique cuts as the training sample but without any
redshift restriction, ensuring full-sky coverage for peculiar velocity
measurement.


\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{MCMC Fit}
\label{sec:mcmc}
The model is fit to the 5,928-galaxy training sample using Hamiltonian Monte
Carlo as implemented in Stan \citep{STAN_html} via the CmdStan interface.
We run 4 chains, each with 1000 warm-up and 1000 sampling iterations.
Two chains converge for the DR1 dataset; the combined posterior uses
2,000 samples.  The mean acceptance rate is $0.938$ and there are no
divergences.

The posterior 68\% confidence intervals for the global TFR parameters are
given in Table~\ref{tab:results}.  The full posterior corner plot is shown
in Fig.~\ref{fig:tophat}.

\begin{table}
    \centering
    \caption{Posterior means and $1\sigma$ uncertainties for the DR1 tophat
    model fit.}
    \label{tab:results}
    \begin{tabular}{lcc}

        \hline
        slope $a$ & $-6.797$ & $\pm 0.071$ \\
        intercept $b$ & $-19.236$ & $\pm 0.017$ \\
        $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x}$ & $0.026$ & $\pm 0.003$ \\
        $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y}$ & $0.422$ & $\pm 0.012$ \\

\end{table}

\section{Peculiar Velocity Catalog}
\label{sec:catalog}
The calibrated tophat model is applied to all galaxies in the MAIN sample
(those satisfying the magnitude and oblique cuts without the redshift
restriction) to produce a peculiar velocity catalog.

\subsection{Output Files}
The catalog is provided as \texttt{tophat\_catalog.fits}, which augments the
input FITS table with five new columns:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{MU\_TF}: the TFR distance modulus residual,
    $\hat{m}_\text{obs} - \bar{m}_\text{pred}$.
    \item \texttt{MU\_ERR}: the combined uncertainty on \texttt{MU\_TF},
    propagated from measurement and intrinsic scatter.
    \item \texttt{LOGDIST}: the log-distance estimate,
    $0.2 \times (\hat{m}_\text{obs} - M_\text{TF})$.
    \item \texttt{LOGDIST\_ERR}: the uncertainty on \texttt{LOGDIST}.
    \item \texttt{MAIN}: a boolean flag indicating whether the galaxy
    satisfies the magnitude and oblique cuts (without redshift restriction).
\end{itemize}

The full posterior predictive covariance matrix among all MAIN-sample
galaxies is provided as \texttt{tophat\_cov.fits}.  This $N\times N$
matrix encodes the correlated uncertainties arising from the shared
TFR calibration parameters and is required for cosmological analyses
that use the full peculiar velocity field.  The covariance matrix
structure is visualized in Fig.~\ref{fig:tophat_cov}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/tophat_cov.png}
    \caption{Visualization of the posterior predictive covariance matrix
    for the MAIN-sample peculiar velocity estimates.  Off-diagonal
    correlations arise from the shared TFR calibration parameters.}
    \label{fig:tophat_cov}
\end{figure}


\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
We have presented a model-based calibration of the Tully-Fisher relation
using the full spiral-galaxy sample from the DESI DR1 Peculiar Velocity survey.
The key results are as follows.
\begin{itemize}
    \item A two-component GMM fit to the DR1 phase space identifies the
    dominant spiral TFR population (weight $w = 0.932$) and defines the
    orientation of oblique selection cuts.  The fiducial selection yields
    a training sample of 5,928 galaxies with a flat pull profile confirming
    no selection-induced bias.
    \item The Stan tophat model, which places a uniform prior on the latent
    log-velocity within the observed selection region, correctly accounts for
    the complex oblique selection boundary without the need for explicit
    bias corrections.
    \item MCMC posterior parameters are slope $a = -6.797 \pm 0.071$,
    intercept $b = -19.236 \pm 0.017$, and intrinsic scatter
    $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x} = 0.026 \pm 0.003$,
    $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y} = 0.422 \pm 0.012$.
    Residual diagnostics in both phase space and redshift show no evidence
    for systematic trends.
    \item The calibrated model is applied to produce a peculiar velocity
    catalog (\texttt{tophat\_catalog.fits}) and a posterior predictive
    covariance matrix (\texttt{tophat\_cov.fits}) for the full MAIN sample.
\end{itemize}

This work is distinguished from other approaches in that it fits a TFR model
rather than fitting a TFR to data.  The difference is highlighted in that
here parameter estimation is based on the model likelihood whereas in other
work a reasonable yet arbitrary merit function is optimized.  A feature of
the TFR model is that it must specify the latent parameter distribution, upon
which we find that the results are sensitive.  An advantage of our approach
is that the model can be used to analyze independent datasets in a
self-consistent manner.

Peculiar velocity measurements from the MAIN catalog, including the study
of large-scale flows and comparison with existing peculiar velocity compilations,
will be presented in a follow-up paper.

\section{Model and Likelihood Derivation}
\label{sec:likelihood}
The data are from observations of a set of TFR galaxies.
Per galaxy there are
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\hat{x}$ the log rotation velocity $\log_{10}(V_\text{rot}/100)$
    with measurement uncertainty $\sigma_x$.
    \item $\hat{y} = M_R$ the absolute $r$-band magnitude with measurement
    uncertainty $\sigma_y$.
\end{itemize}
The galaxy set undergoes sample selection through the oblique cuts described
in \S\ref{sec:selection}.  The selection boundaries in $(x,\,y)$ space are
characterized by the slope $s_\text{plane}$ and intercepts $c_1$, $c_2$
of the two oblique boundaries, plus the magnitude limits $\hat{y}_\text{min}$
and $\hat{y}_\text{max}$.

The probability of observations given a model with parameter set $\Theta$ is
\begin{equation}
\prod_{i=1}^{N} p(\hat{x}_i,\hat{y}_i|\Theta),
\end{equation}
where all measurements of all $N$ galaxies are taken to be independent.
$\Theta$ includes global parameters that characterize the TFR:
the slope $a$, intercept $b$, intrinsic scatter $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x}$
and $\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y}$.

There is a set of latent parameters used in calculating the probability of
the observations.  The latent parameters are the per-object
\begin{itemize}
\item True log-velocity $x$, the latent TFR variable.
\item True absolute magnitude $y = a\,x + b + \varepsilon_y$,
where $\varepsilon_y$ is drawn from the intrinsic scatter distribution.
\item The offset $\delta\phi$ between the fiber position angle and the
true galaxy major axis.
\end{itemize}

The model specifies each term as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $p(\delta\phi|.) = \text{Cauchy}(\delta\phi;\,\gamma)$.
    The uncertainty in fiber placement deduced from galaxies common to SGA
    and MaNGA, with $\gamma = 4.217^\circ$.
    \item $p(\varepsilon_y|.) = \mathcal{N}(\varepsilon_y;\,\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y})$.
    Intrinsic scatter along the $y$ axis.
    \item $p(x|.) = \text{Uniform}(x;\,x_\text{min},\,x_\text{max})$.
    The latent true log-velocity is drawn from a tophat (uniform) prior
    within the selection region.  The boundaries $x_\text{min}$, $x_\text{max}$
    are determined by the oblique selection cuts evaluated at the observed $y$.
    \item $p(y|x,\varepsilon_y) = \delta_D(y - (a\,x + b + \varepsilon_y))$.
    Deterministic TFR relationship.
    \item $p(\hat{x}|.) = \mathcal{N}_T(\hat{x};\,V\sec(\delta\phi)/100,\,\sigma_x;\,
    x_\text{sel})$.
    Observed log-velocity is a truncated Gaussian at the selection boundary,
    where $V$ is the expected rotation velocity at the fiber position and
    the factor $\sec(\delta\phi)$ accounts for the fiber placement offset.
    \item $p(\hat{y}|.) = \mathcal{N}_T(\hat{y};\,y,\,\sigma_y;\,\hat{y}_\text{min},\hat{y}_\text{max})$.
    Observed magnitude is a truncated Gaussian at the selection boundaries.
\end{itemize}

Putting all this together, the likelihood for a single galaxy is
\begin{align}
    p(\hat{x},\hat{y}|\Theta) &=
    \int
    \mathcal{N}_T\!\left(\hat{x} - \frac{x\sec(\delta\phi)}{1};\,\sigma_x,\,x_\text{sel}\right)
    \mathcal{N}_T(\hat{y} - (ax+b+\varepsilon_y);\,\sigma_y,\,\hat{y}_\text{min},\hat{y}_\text{max})
    \nonumber \\
    &\quad\times
    \text{Uniform}(x;\,x_\text{min},\,x_\text{max})\,
    \mathcal{N}(\varepsilon_y;\,\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y})\,
    \text{Cauchy}(\delta\phi;\,\gamma)
    \;dx\,d\varepsilon_y\,d\delta\phi.
\end{align}
There are $2N_\text{gal}$ latent parameters $\{x,\,\varepsilon_y\}$ and
4 global parameters $\{a,\,b,\,\sigma_{\mathrm{int},x},\,\sigma_{\mathrm{int},y}\}$.
The $\delta\phi$ integral is evaluated numerically using a sum over
Cauchy quantiles.

\subsection{Inferring Peculiar Velocities}
A galaxy has intrinsic parameters $\mu$ (distance modulus),
$x_\text{true}$ (true log-velocity), and $\varepsilon_y$ (TFR scatter).
These are inferred from observations $\hat{x}$, $\hat{y}$, and the
TFR parameters calibrated in the training stage.

The TFR-predicted apparent magnitude at observed redshift $z_\text{obs}$ is
$m_\text{pred} = a\,x_\text{true} + b + \mu(z_\text{obs})$.
The peculiar velocity distance modulus residual is
\begin{equation}
    \mu_\text{TF} = \hat{m}_\text{obs} - m_\text{pred},
\end{equation}
which is nonzero when the galaxy's true distance differs from that implied
by its observed redshift (i.e., when it has a peculiar velocity).

Sample selection cuts are applied, and only galaxies satisfying the MAIN
selection (magnitude and oblique cuts, no $z$ restriction) are included in
the catalog.  The likelihood for a detected galaxy with observables $\hat{x}$
and $\hat{y}$ is
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}(\mu;\,\hat{x},\hat{y}) &=
    \frac{f(\hat{x},\hat{y}|\mu)}{p_\text{det}(\mu)},
\end{align}
where $p_\text{det}$ is the probability of the galaxy satisfying the
selection given its distance modulus $\mu$.
